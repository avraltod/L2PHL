01M01 Roster — Who lives here
Population structure

A young, growing nation — with 40.56% under 20

The Philippines is a young country. The largest single age cohort is 10–19 year-olds (22.66%), followed by those under 10 (17.9%). Only 3.6% are over 70. The demographic dividend is real — but it requires education, jobs, and social infrastructure to convert into prosperity.

The median age is 25. Mindanao is the youngest region with 19.66% under 10, while NCR is the oldest with just 14.67% in that bracket. Urban areas skew slightly older, reflecting internal migration patterns.

Household composition

Modal household: a head, spouse, and children

43.22% of members are sons or daughters of the household head. 21.66% are heads themselves. The extended family is common — 9.36% of members are grandchildren, and another 3.97% are other relatives.

56.23% of all members are single or unmarried — reflecting the young age structure. Among those aged 18+, marriage rates vary sharply: common-law/live-in arrangements are more prevalent in urban areas (14.1%) than rural (10.7%).

Women outlive men: 6.0% of women are widowed vs. just 1.37% of men — a significant care-work implication for ageing policy.

Disability

2.51% live with disability — but 41.7% have no PWD ID

2.51% of household members have a disability, representing approximately 2.7 million Filipinos. Men show a higher prevalence (3.08%) than women (1.92%). Rural areas have slightly higher rates (2.65%) than urban (2.41%).

Of those with a disability, 58.3% have a PWD ID card — but 41.7% do not. The gap is widest in Mindanao, where only 47.8% of PWDs hold an ID, potentially leaving them excluded from disability benefits, PhilHealth discounts, and priority services.

02M02 Education — Learning
School attendance

78.32% of 5–24 year-olds are in school — but attendance collapses at 18

Attendance is near-universal in primary years: 97.66% for ages 6–11, and 96.33% for ages 12–15. But at 16–17 it falls to 87.25%, and at 18–19 it plummets to 65.21%. By 20–24, only 30.19% are still in school.

Girls attend at higher rates than boys (80.4% vs 76.3%) — a consistent gender advantage that runs through all age bands. Mindanao actually has the highest attendance rate (80.6%) across macro-regions.

89.81% of students attend public schools. Private school attendance is highest in NCR at 14.3%, and lowest in Visayas at 8.8% — reflecting urban wealth concentration.

Why do young people leave school?

Employment is the top reason (24.6%), followed by financial cost (15.7%), and having finished schooling (14.9%). Marriage accounts for 6.1% of non-attendance — almost entirely female.

Educational attainment

Half of adults stopped at secondary level. Women outpace men at college.

Among adults 18+, 50.73% have secondary as their highest attainment. Only 13.36% have completed college — though women (15.5%) outperform men (11.2%) at that level. Just 0.45% hold postgraduate degrees.

Intergenerational mobility is limited: 55.92% of fathers and 53.26% of mothers attained only primary education. The path from primary-educated parents to college-educated children is the central educational mobility challenge.

Annual education spend per student: ₱16,973. Urban families spend more (₱18,004) than rural (₱15,636). School fees show the biggest urban-rural gap: ₱4,062 vs ₱2,048 — reflecting higher private school enrolment in cities.

03M03 Employment — Working
Labour market

52.1% work. But jobs without protection dominate.

The employment rate is 52.1% for adults 15+. But the gender gap is stark: men at 64.6% vs women at 39.4% — a 25 percentage-point chasm that reflects unpaid care work, cultural norms, and structural exclusion.

Peak employment is at 45–54 (73.7%). Youth (15–24) employment is just 22.4% — but many are still in school, making direct comparison difficult. NCR leads at 53.2%; Visayas lags at 47.7%.

Informality

More than four in five workers have no contract.

83.24% of employed workers have no formal contract. Women fare worse (76.9%) than men (68.2%). Only 17.83% have a written contract — mostly in government and private establishment roles.

Pension/social security contributions are paid by employers for just 28.38% of workers. NCR is best at 48.2%; Mindanao worst at 18.7%. Among those with benefits, 42.85% report none at all — followed by PhilHealth (23.6%) and SSS/GSIS (22.3%).

Gig work is modest but present at 5.88%. The average working week is 31.3 hours — pulled down by the 41.3% who work fewer than 20 hours.

Job loss & search

10.1% of households had a member lose a job in the past 30 days. 17.6% of working-age adults are actively looking for a new job, and another 2.85% want more work.

04M04–M05 Income & Finance — Money
Income

66% get regular income — but mean 6-month earnings are just ₱11,618

66.07% of individuals received regular income in the past 6 months, and 85.94% received at least some seasonal or occasional income. But the amounts are modest: the mean 6-month cash earnings is ₱11,618 — roughly ₱1,936 per month.

Urban workers earn 14% more than rural (₱12,266 vs ₱10,759). NCR earns the most (₱12,574) while Visayas is close behind (₱11,695). Luzon outside NCR and Mindanao cluster around ₱11,300–₱11,600.

7.37% of households receive OFW remittances — averaging ₱21,845 over 6 months, nearly twice the average wage earner's income. 22.82% received domestic support from relatives or relief organizations. Just 8.1% receive pension income.

Financial inclusion

Mobile money arrived. Banks haven't.

Only 4.95% of households have a formal bank account — placing the Philippines far below regional peers. The gap is widest in Visayas, where just 1.48% of households are banked.

Mobile money (18.0%) has outpaced banking significantly, but even this is uneven: rural households lag urban by 5.6 percentage points. NCR reaches 25.5% mobile money penetration — rural Visayas is the other end of the spectrum.

Paradoxically, rural households save at slightly higher rates (14.5%) than urban (11.7%) — likely through informal mechanisms like paluwagan savings groups (8.2% nationally). The financial system has not yet caught up with how ordinary Filipinos actually manage money.

Borrowing (18.1%) outpaces saving (12.87%) by 5 percentage points. Many households are net borrowers — managing cash flow through debt rather than savings buffers.

Government support

23.1% receive domestic support — CCT reaches 1 in 4

23.1% of households received gift, support, assistance, or relief from domestic sources in the past 6 months. Government institutions are the single largest provider (33.6% of recipients), followed by the CCT/Pantawid/4Ps programme (25.3%).

NCR sees the highest government support rate (54.6% of recipients), while Mindanao relies more on AKAP (23.0%) and AICS (18.8%). Pension and social security programmes reach 6.9% of all households.

05M06 Migration — On the move
Migration

35.6% are thinking about leaving — NCR most of all

While only 0.05% of individuals have actually moved in the past (the survey sample is largely resident), 35.57% of working-age adults are considering migrating within the next year. The overwhelmingly dominant reason for past moves: employment and job relocation.

NCR — the capital — shows the highest migration intent at 45.49%. Despite better services, wages, and connectivity, nearly half of Manila's working-age adults are thinking of leaving. Visayas shows the lowest intent at 22.62%, despite having the most economic disadvantages.

Women (37.9%) are slightly more likely to be considering migration than men (32.96%). Urban dwellers show marginally higher intent (36.2%) than rural (34.6%).

06M07 Health — Getting sick
Health system

53.9% have no PhilHealth. When hospitalization hits, a ₱52,000 bill arrives — half paid out-of-pocket.

54% of members have no PhilHealth coverage at all. Of the 46.1% who are covered, most are non-paying members or dependents. Only 15.7% are paying PhilHealth members in their own right.

Coverage is starkly unequal: Visayas at just 35.6% vs NCR at 52.6%. Rural and urban coverage is essentially the same (~46%), suggesting the gap is regional, not urban-rural.

When care is needed: 32.4% go to a Barangay Health Station, 27.3% to a public hospital, 20.0% to a Rural Health Unit. Private options account for only 20.3% of visits — but they absorb far more household spending.

9.18% of members were hospitalized in the past 3 months. The mean hospital bill is ₱52,160, of which ₱23,931 (46%) is paid out-of-pocket. For a household earning ₱11,618 in 6 months, that is a full year's income.

Health access & child nutrition

1 in 3 needed health care — but only 38% got it.

33.3% of household members needed health services in the past 30 days. Of those, only 38.0% were able to access care — a 62% unmet need rate. NCR fares best (47.7% access) while Visayas struggles most (26.1%).

The top reason for not accessing care is cost: 42.6% cite inability to afford it. Visayas is hardest hit, where 64.2% of those denied care blame cost — more than double the NCR rate (27.5%).

Among children under 5, malnutrition remains widespread: 26.6% are stunted (low height-for-age), 18.2% are underweight, and 18.0% are wasted. Boys show slightly higher stunting (29.8%) than girls (22.9%). Visayas has the highest stunting rate (32.0%) and severe stunting (18.4%).

07M08 Food & Nutrition — Eating
Food access

The wet market rules. 99.6% pay in cash.

73.97% of households buy most of their food from wet markets. Sari-sari stores are second at 15.06%, while modern retail (supermarkets and large chains) account for just 6.7% combined. The Philippine food system is profoundly local and informal.

83.69% receive no receipt when shopping. 99.61% pay in cash — digital payments (GCash/Maya/QR) account for just 0.13%. The food economy is almost entirely cash-based and undocumented.

The average travel time to the main food source is 17.6 minutes. 43.25% shop more than once a week; 40.45% go once a week. The wet market is a routine, daily fixture of Filipino life.

The SSB paradox

Consumption is lowest among young children (<10: 78.6%) and the elderly (65+: 82.8%), but peaks sharply in working-age adults (25–44: 94.3%). This is not a childhood habit — it is a working-age norm.

Sugary beverages

Almost 9 in 10 drink sugar-sweetened beverages — 6.9 servings a day

89.38% of individuals consume sweetened sugary beverages. The average consumer drinks 6.9 single-serve portions daily. This pattern is essentially uniform across urban (88.94%) and rural (89.88%) settings — it is a national dietary norm, not a segmented one.

At the household level, 89.92% of households have at least one SSB consumer. This means the category of "SSB-free household" is a statistical minority (10.1%).

The implications for non-communicable disease burden — diabetes, hypertension, dental caries, obesity — are significant. The Philippines already faces a rising NCD burden, and the near-universal SSB consumption is a major dietary risk factor.

08M09 Natural Hazards — When disasters strike
Climate & disasters

76% hit by typhoon. 50% by extreme heat. 40% by earthquake. Half got no warning.

The Philippines sits at the intersection of the world's most active typhoon belt, the Pacific Ring of Fire, and the increasingly severe El Niño/La Niña cycle. The survey confirms multi-hazard exposure is the norm, not the exception.

76.38% of households experienced a typhoon in the past 3 years. 49.7% faced extreme heat. 40.57% felt an earthquake. 36.4% were flooded. These are not once-in-a-generation events — they are annual occurrences that households must absorb without meaningful insurance or institutional support.

Only 55.11% received early warning before a hazard event. Of those who did, a reassuring 97.39% said they understood the message — suggesting the problem is coverage, not comprehension. The goal should be universal warning reach, not message redesign.

The warning gap

Of every 100 households facing a hazard: 55 receive a warning — 45 do not. The 45 who receive no warning are disproportionately rural, remote, and in Mindanao and Visayas.

11M13 Assets & Energy — What families own
Household assets

Motorcycles and washing machines — but only 3.9% own a car

The two most common household assets are almost exactly tied: washing machines (54.98%) and motorcycles/tricycles (54.03%). These reflect the everyday priorities of Filipino family life — mobility and laundry.

Refrigerators reach 48.96% nationally. NCR leads (58.8%) while Visayas lags (42.9%). Computer/laptop ownership is 14.59% nationally — but 24.84% in NCR vs just 9.05% in Visayas. This digital asset gap directly constrains remote work and educational access.

Air conditioning is 10.47% nationally — almost entirely an NCR phenomenon (24.4% there vs 5.8% in Mindanao and 8.5% in Visayas). Cars are 3.93% nationally — equally rare across all regions (3.4–4.9%).

Energy for cooking

LPG on top — but fuelwood burns in half of homes

LPG is the primary cooking fuel at 61.49%. But 50.73% of households still use fuelwood — not as a curiosity but as a primary or supplementary fuel. 27.03% use charcoal. These are not mutually exclusive: many households use multiple fuels depending on what they are cooking and what is available.

Electricity for cooking is 21.38% nationally — highest in NCR (28.7%) and lowest in Visayas (15.2%). Despite near-universal electrification, the grid has not displaced solid fuels for cooking in most of the country.

12M14 Views & Perceptions — What they think
Perceptions & sentiment

Life satisfaction: 3.04/5. 34.7% feel worse off. AI anxiety tops all concerns.

Life satisfaction averages 3.04 out of 5 (where 1 is most satisfied). 52.27% are neutral at 3. Only 25.3% report being satisfied or very satisfied; 22.5% are dissatisfied or very dissatisfied.

On the economic situation: 46.32% say it is the same as last month. 34.71% feel worse off (somewhat or much worse). Only 18.98% feel things improved. Visayas is most pessimistic (39.4% worse) while Mindanao is most stable (51.5% same).

When asked about their income group, most place themselves in the middle: 48.62% group 3, 29.72% group 2, 20.21% group 1. Only 1.45% place themselves in the top two groups — suggesting the rich are either absent from the survey population or reluctant to self-identify as wealthy.

Neighbourhood safety: 62.4% feel unsafe walking in their area. Only 10.6% feel safe or very safe. This is a striking finding given the relatively low crime-rate narrative of many Philippine localities.

Attitudes & concerns

AI anxiety is the top fear — above jobs, education, and politics
