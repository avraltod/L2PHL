Population structure

A young, growing nation — with 41% under 20

The Philippines is a young country. The largest single age cohort is 10–19 year-olds at 23 percent, followed by children under 10 at 18 percent while only 4 percent of the population is over 70. With a median age of 25, the demographic dividend is real — but realizing it depends on delivering education, jobs, and social infrastructure to translate into broad-based prosperity.

This is not uniform across the country. Mindanao is the youngest, with 20 percent of its population under 10, while NCR has the smallest share in that bracket at 15 percent – a gap consistent with long-standing patterns of internal migration toward urban centers, which tend to skew slightly older.

Household composition

Modal household: a head, spouse, and children

Four in ten Filipino households (40 percent) follow the classic nuclear pattern: a head, spouse, and children. But nearly as many (39 percent) are extended families — households that include grandchildren, siblings, parents, or other relatives alongside the nuclear core. The average household has 4 members.

Single-parent households — a head with children but no spouse — account for 8 percent, while couples without children make up 8 percent. Just 5 percent of households are single-person.

Marital status mirrors the country's young age structure. Over half of all household members are single or unmarried. Women are far more likely to be widowed than men — 6 percent versus 1 percent — pointing to longer female life expectancy and the disproportionate care burdens that older women bear.

Disability

3% live with disability — but 42% have no PWD ID

An estimated 3 percent of household members — approximately 3 million Filipinos — have a disability. Prevalence is higher among men (3 percent) than women (2 percent), and slightly higher in rural areas (3 percent) than in urban (2 percent).

What the numbers also reveal is a significant gap in formal recognition. While 58 percent of persons with disabilities hold a PWD ID card, 42 percent do not — meaning that for nearly half of this population, access to disability benefits, PhilHealth discounts, and priority services may be out of reach. The gap is most acute in Mindanao, where fewer than half of PWDs hold an ID, suggesting that geographic and administrative barriers compound the challenge of reaching those who need support most.

Migration

2% of households have someone thinking about leaving — but among them, intent is strong

Migration intent is narrow but concentrated. Only 2 percent of households report at least one member aged 15 or older considering migration within the next year, translating to about 1 percent of the adult population. The overwhelmingly dominant reason for past moves: employment and job relocation.

Within those households, 36 percent of individual members are the ones actively considering the move. Women (1 percent) are slightly more likely to be considering migration than men (1 percent).

Among those with a stated destination, two-thirds intend to go abroad and one-third to stay within the Philippines. The United States is the top overseas destination (18 percent of abroad-bound), followed by Australia (11 percent), the United Kingdom (8 percent), South Korea (8 percent), Japan (8 percent), Saudi Arabia (5 percent), and Canada (4 percent).

02M02 Education — Learning
School attendance

78% of 5–24 year-olds are in school — but only 65% continue after K-12 graduation

The K-12 system keeps students in school through senior high. Attendance is near-universal at elementary level (98 percent for ages 6–11) and stays high through junior high (96 percent for 12–15). Senior high school (ages 16–17) holds at 87 percent. The sharp transition comes at 18–19 — the post-K-12 years — where only 65 percent continue to tertiary education. By 20–24, just 30 percent remain enrolled.

Girls outperform boys at every stage beyond elementary. The gender gap widens with age: at 16–17 girls attend at 93 percent versus boys at 82 percent, and at 18–19 the gap is 71 percent versus 60 percent. Overall, girls attend at 80 percent compared to boys at 76 percent — a consistent 4 percentage-point advantage.

Ninety percent of students attend public schools. Private school attendance is highest in NCR at 14 percent, and lowest in Visayas at 9 percent — reflecting urban wealth concentration.

Why do young people leave school? Employment is the top reason (26 percent), followed by financial constraints (17 percent), having finished schooling (16 percent), and family matters (11 percent). Lack of interest and job-seeking each account for about 8 percent.

Educational attainment

Half of adults stopped at secondary level. Women outpace men at college.

Among adults 18 and older, 51 percent have secondary as their highest attainment. Only 13 percent have completed college — though women (16 percent) outperform men (11 percent) at that level. Less than 1 percent hold postgraduate degrees.

Intergenerational mobility is limited: 65 percent of fathers and 61 percent of mothers attained only primary education. The path from primary-educated parents to college-educated children is the central educational mobility challenge.

Annual education spend per student averages ₱15,261 (median ₱8,600). Urban families spend more — mean ₱15,742 (median ₱8,900) versus rural ₱14,731 (median ₱8,500). School fees show the biggest urban-rural gap: ₱3,034 vs ₱2,059 — reflecting higher private school enrolment in cities. (Top 1% of each component excluded.)

Early childhood

Only 24% of children 0–4 attend daycare or kindergarten

Early childhood education attendance rises sharply with age: fewer than 1 percent of children at ages 0–1 are enrolled, rising to 31 percent at age 3 and 69 percent at age 4. The main reason for non-attendance is being considered too young (26 percent of non-attenders aged 0–4).

03M03 Employment — Working
Labour market

45% work. But jobs without protection dominate.

The employment rate stands at 45 percent for adults 15 and older — but that headline figure masks a sharp gender divide. Men participate at 59 percent, women at 30 percent, a 29 percentage-point gap that reflects not just cultural norms but the structural weight of unpaid care work and limited access to formal employment for women.

Employment peaks at ages 45–54, where 64 percent are working. Youth employment (ages 15–24) appears low at 19 percent, though this figure requires context: many young people in this age band are still in school, making a direct comparison with older cohorts misleading. Regionally, Luzon leads at 48 percent while the Visayas lags at 41 percent.

Job loss & search

10 percent of households had a member lose a job in the past 30 days. 3 percent of working-age adults are actively looking for a new job, and another 1 percent want more work.

Informality

About three in four workers have no contract.

Informality is the norm in the Philippine labor market. Seventy-two percent of employed workers have no formal contract. Only 18 percent hold a written contract, concentrated largely in government and formal private sector roles.

The absence of contracts translates directly into an absence of protection. Employers contribute to pension or social security for just 28 percent of workers — ranging from 48 percent in NCR to 22 percent in Visayas, a gap that maps closely onto the geographic concentration of formal employment. Among workers reporting on benefits, PhilHealth (34 percent) and SSS/GSIS (31 percent) are the most commonly reported where coverage exists while 60 percent receive none.

Working hours tell a related story. The average working week is 31 hours (median 35) — pulled down considerably by the 42 percent of workers putting in fewer than 20 hours. Gig work accounts for 5 percent of employment, modest but worth tracking as platform-based work expands. (Hours: top 1% excluded.)

04M04–M05 Income & Finance — Money
Income sources

66% receive regular income — OFW remittances reach 9 percent of households

Sixty-six percent of individuals received regular income in the past 6 months. Mean 6-month cash earnings are ₱10,576 (median ₱7,500; top 1 percent excluded, 28 obs above ₱96,000). The sources of income are diverse: wages and salaries dominate, but non-wage income streams play a critical role for many households.

Nine percent of households receive OFW remittances from family members working abroad, with a mean remittance of ₱19,497 (median ₱10,000; top 1 percent excluded). Twenty-three percent received domestic support from relatives or relief organizations. Seven percent receive pension income — reflecting the limited reach of formal retirement systems in a country where most work is informal.

Financial inclusion

Mobile money arrived. Banks haven't.

Only 5 percent of households have a formal bank account — placing the Philippines far below regional peers. The gap is widest in Visayas, where just 1 percent of households are banked.

Mobile money (18 percent) has outpaced banking significantly, but even this is uneven. NCR reaches 26 percent mobile money penetration; Visayas trails at just 12 percent.

Rural households save at slightly higher rates (14 percent) than urban (12 percent) — likely through informal mechanisms like paluwagan savings groups (8 percent nationally). The financial system has not yet caught up with how ordinary Filipinos actually manage money.

Borrowing (18 percent) outpaces saving (13 percent) by 5 percentage points. Many households are net borrowers — managing cash flow through debt rather than savings buffers. Of borrowers, 94 percent use formal sources; only 6 percent borrow informally.

05M07 Health — Getting sick
Health system

54% have no PhilHealth. When hospitalization hits, a ₱46,834 bill arrives — 18.5% paid out-of-pocket.

The health service access gap has a financial explanation. Fifty-four percent of household members have no PhilHealth coverage — and of the 46 percent who do, most are non-paying members or dependents. Only 17 percent are contributing members.

06M08 Food & Nutrition — Eating
Food access

The wet market rules. 99.6% pay in cash.

The Philippine food system is profoundly local and informal. Nearly three in four households buy most of their food from wet markets, with sari-sari stores a distant second at 15 percent. Modern retail — supermarkets and large chains combined — accounts for just 7 percent.

The transactions that happen in these markets leave almost no trace. Eighty-four percent of shoppers receive no receipt, and nearly 100 percent pay in cash. Digital payments — GCash, Maya, QR codes — account for less than 1 percent of food purchases. This is an economy that runs on physical money and personal trust, not platforms.

Food shopping is also a frequent, routine part of daily life. Forty-three percent shop more than once a week, and another 40 percent go once a week. The wet market is not an errand — it is a rhythm.

07M09 Natural Hazards — When disasters strike
Climate & disasters

76% hit by typhoon. 50% by extreme heat. 40% by earthquake. Half got no warning.

The Philippines is one of the world's most disaster-prone countries, situated at the convergence of the Pacific typhoon belt, the Ring of Fire, and an increasingly volatile El Niño/La Niña cycle. For Filipino households, multi-hazard exposure is not the exception — it is the baseline condition.

Three in four households experienced a typhoon in the past three years. Half were affected by extreme heat events. Four in ten reported earthquake exposure. More than a third experienced flooding. These are not isolated shocks — they are recurring, overlapping hazards that erode household welfare, deplete assets, and set back human capital accumulation, particularly among the poor and vulnerable.

Early warning systems represent a critical gap in disaster risk management. Only 55 percent of affected households received advance warning before a hazard event. Among those who did, 85 percent reported understanding the message — a good but imperfect rate that suggests room for improvement in both coverage and clarity. Strengthening last-mile warning delivery should be a priority for disaster risk reduction investments.
