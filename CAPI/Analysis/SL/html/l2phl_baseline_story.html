Population structure

A young, growing nation — with 41% under 20

The Philippines is a young country. The largest single age cohort is 10–19 year-olds at 23 percent, followed by children under 10 at 18 percent while only 4 percent of the population is over 70. With a median age of 25, the demographic dividend is real — but realizing it depends on delivering education, jobs, and social infrastructure to translate into broad-based prosperity.

This is not uniform across the country. Mindanao is the youngest, with 20 percent of its population under 10, while NCR has the smallest share in that bracket at 15 percent – a gap consistent with long-standing patterns of internal migration toward urban centers, which tend to skew slightly older.

Household composition

Modal household: a head, spouse, and children

Four in ten Filipino households (40 percent) follow the classic nuclear pattern: a head, spouse, and children. But nearly as many (39 percent) are extended families — households that include grandchildren, siblings, parents, or other relatives alongside the nuclear core. The average household has 4 members.

Single-parent households — a head with children but no spouse — account for 8 percent, while couples without children make up 8 percent. Just 5 percent of households are single-person.

Marital status mirrors the country's young age structure. Over half of all household members are single or unmarried. Women are far more likely to be widowed than men — 6 percent versus 1 percent — pointing to longer female life expectancy and the disproportionate care burdens that older women bear.

Disability

3% live with disability — but 42% have no PWD ID

An estimated 3 percent of household members — approximately 3 million Filipinos — have a disability. Prevalence is higher among men (3 percent) than women (2 percent), and slightly higher in rural areas (3 percent) than in urban (2 percent).

What the numbers also reveal is a significant gap in formal recognition. While 58 percent of persons with disabilities hold a PWD ID card, 42 percent do not — meaning that for nearly half of this population, access to disability benefits, PhilHealth discounts, and priority services may be out of reach. The gap is most acute in Mindanao, where fewer than half of PWDs hold an ID, suggesting that geographic and administrative barriers compound the challenge of reaching those who need support most.

Migration

2% of households have someone thinking about leaving — but among them, intent is strong

Migration intent is narrow but concentrated. Only 2 percent of households report at least one member aged 15 or older considering migration within the next year, translating to about 1 percent of the adult population. The overwhelmingly dominant reason for past moves: employment and job relocation.

Within those households, 36 percent of individual members are the ones actively considering the move.

Among those with a stated destination, two-thirds intend to go abroad and one-third to stay within the Philippines. The United States is the top overseas destination (18 percent of abroad-bound), followed by Australia (11 percent), the United Kingdom (8 percent), South Korea (8 percent), Japan (8 percent), Saudi Arabia (5 percent), and Canada (4 percent).

02M02 Education — Learning
School attendance

78% of 5–24 year-olds are in school — but only 65% continue after K-12 graduation

The K-12 system keeps students in school through senior high. Attendance is near-universal at elementary level (98 percent for ages 6–11) and stays high through junior high (96 percent for 12–15). Senior high school (ages 16–17) holds at 87 percent. The sharp transition comes at 18–19 — the post-K-12 years — where only 65 percent continue to tertiary education. By 20–24, just 30 percent remain enrolled.

Girls outperform boys at every stage beyond elementary. The gender gap widens with age: at 16–17 girls attend at 93 percent versus boys at 82 percent, and at 18–19 the gap is 71 percent versus 60 percent. Overall, girls attend at 80 percent compared to boys at 76 percent — a consistent 4 percentage-point advantage.

Ninety percent of students attend public schools. Private school attendance is highest in NCR at 14 percent, and lowest in Visayas at 9 percent — reflecting urban wealth concentration.

Why do young people leave school? Employment is the top reason (26 percent), followed by financial constraints (15 percent), having finished schooling (16 percent), family matters (11 percent), and distance to school (10 percent). Taken together, cost and domestic burden — not academic failure — are the dominant drivers of dropout.

Educational attainment

Half of adults stopped at secondary level. Women outpace men at college.

Among adults 18 and older, 51 percent have secondary as their highest attainment. Only 13 percent have completed college — though women (16 percent) outperform men (11 percent) at that level. Less than 1 percent hold postgraduate degrees.

Intergenerational mobility is limited: 65 percent of fathers and 61 percent of mothers attained only primary education. The path from primary-educated parents to college-educated children is the central educational mobility challenge.

Annual education spend per student averages ₱16,973 — urban families spend ₱18,005 versus rural ₱15,834. School fees show the biggest urban-rural gap, reflecting higher private school enrolment in cities.

Early childhood

Only 24% of children 0–4 attend daycare or kindergarten

Early childhood education attendance rises sharply with age: fewer than 1 percent of children at ages 0–1 are enrolled, rising to 31 percent at age 3 and 69 percent at age 4. Among those attending, 66 percent are in daycare or nursery programmes, while 34 percent attend kindergarten. The main reason for non-attendance is being considered too young (26 percent of non-attenders aged 0–4).

The Philippine food system is profoundly local and informal. Nearly three in four households buy most of their food from wet markets, with sari-sari stores a distant second at 15 percent. Modern retail — supermarkets and large chains combined — accounts for just 7 percent.

09M13 Assets & Energy — What families own
Household assets

Motorcycles and washing machines — but only 3.9% own a car

The two most common household assets are almost exactly tied: washing machines (55 percent) and motorcycles/tricycles (54 percent). These reflect the everyday priorities of Filipino family life — mobility and laundry.

Refrigerators reach 49 percent nationally. NCR leads (59 percent) while Visayas lags (45 percent). Computer/laptop ownership is 15 percent nationally — but 25 percent in NCR vs just 9 percent in Visayas. This digital asset gap directly constrains remote work and educational access.

Air conditioning is 10 percent nationally — almost entirely an NCR phenomenon (24 percent there vs 6 percent in Mindanao and 9 percent in Visayas). Cars are 4 percent nationally — equally rare across all regions (3 to 5 percent).

Energy for cooking

LPG on top — but fuelwood burns in half of homes

LPG is the primary cooking fuel at 61 percent. But 51 percent of households still use fuelwood — not as a curiosity but as a primary or supplementary fuel. Twenty-seven percent use charcoal. These are not mutually exclusive: many households use multiple fuels depending on what they are cooking and what is available.

The regional split is dramatic: 94 percent of NCR households use LPG vs just 39 percent in Mindanao. Conversely, 72 percent of Mindanao households use fuelwood vs only 4 percent in NCR. Visayas and Mindanao together have the highest biomass dependency, with direct implications for indoor air quality and deforestation.

Electricity for cooking is 21 percent nationally — highest in NCR (29 percent) and lowest in Visayas (15 percent). Despite near-universal electrification, the grid has not displaced solid fuels for cooking in most of the country.

10M14 Views & Perceptions — What they think
Perceptions & sentiment

Life satisfaction: 3.04/5. 19% feel worse off. Prices and governance top all concerns.

Life satisfaction averages 3.04 out of 5 (where 1 is "not satisfied at all" and 5 is "completely satisfied"). Fifty-two percent select "satisfied" (code 3). Twenty-three percent report being more than satisfied or completely satisfied (codes 4-5); 25 percent are only partly satisfied or not satisfied at all (codes 1-2). Note: the scale has no neutral midpoint — code 3 is a positive response ("satisfied").

On the economic situation: 46 percent say it is the same as last month. Nineteen percent feel worse off (somewhat or much worse). Thirty-five percent feel things improved. Visayas is most pessimistic (20 percent worse) while Mindanao is most stable (51 percent same).

When asked about their income group, most place themselves in the middle: 49 percent group 3, 30 percent group 2, 20 percent group 1. Only 1 percent place themselves in the top two groups — suggesting the rich are either absent from the survey population or reluctant to self-identify as wealthy.

Neighbourhood safety: 11 percent feel unsafe walking in their area. Eighty percent feel safe or very safe — though the remaining 10 percent report being afraid to walk alone, a distinct dimension beyond the safe/unsafe binary.

Attitudes & concerns

Prices and governance top all concerns — AI anxiety ranks lower than expected

Seventy-seven percent of households want more say in government, 76 percent agree prices are rising too quickly, and 71 percent worry about their children's education. Political instability concerns 64 percent. AI or automation displacing jobs — at 52 percent — ranks well below these bread-and-butter anxieties.

This is striking context: in a country where 72 percent of workers have no contract and formal employment protection is minimal, the perceived threat of AI arrives as an additional layer of insecurity on top of already precarious conditions — but it is not the dominant worry.

The most cautious finding: only 37 percent believe taxes are well-spent — the lowest agreement rate among the 11 Likert statements. Whether this reflects genuine distrust in government or informed awareness of public finance quality is a deeper question.

Optimism about the economy (55 percent) and belief that now is a good time to find a job (56 percent) are essentially equal — suggesting moderate confidence, consistent with the broader picture of a labour market that is active but precarious.

Regional sentiment

Visayas most pessimistic. Mindanao most stable.

Visayas shows the highest "worse off" sentiment at 20 percent, consistent with Visayas being the region with the lowest PhilHealth coverage, lowest internet access, lowest financial inclusion, and highest hazard exposure relative to infrastructure.

Mindanao shows the highest "same" rate at 51 percent — suggesting a stability perception despite lower objective indicators. Visayas is most optimistic with 39 percent reporting improvement, while NCR trails at 33 percent improved.

Income mobility aspirations

Where families are vs. where they want their children to be

The Sankey diagram shows the flow between self-assessed income class (left) and expected income class for one's children (right). Each band's width is proportional to the share of households in that combination.

The dominant flow is from "Middle" self-classification (48%) staying at "Middle" for children (36% of all HHs). But there is clear upward aspiration: families in groups 1 and 2 overwhelmingly expect their children to land in group 3 or higher.

Almost no one places themselves in the top two groups (1.5% combined), while 19% expect their children to reach group 4 or 5 — revealing a strong upward mobility aspiration even among the poorest families.
