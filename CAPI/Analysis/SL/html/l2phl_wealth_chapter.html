Welfare Measurement

When income data fails — assets tell the story

The L2Phl CAPI baseline posed an immediate challenge: the income module (M04) contained critical translation errors that rendered monetary values unusable for analysis. Rather than abandon poverty measurement, we pivoted to the most reliable alternative — observable household assets and living conditions.

This is not a weakness. International welfare economics increasingly recognizes that assets, housing quality, and infrastructure access often better reflect long-term household welfare than income reported in a single survey round. They capture material living standards more honestly than respondents' memory of annual earnings.

We developed five distinct statistical methods, each using 40–77 household indicators. All ranked 2,470 households from poorest (Q1) to wealthiest (Q5). The results converged: geography, education, and asset ownership drive Philippine poverty patterns with remarkable consistency across methods.

The Ridge PMT (Method 5) emerged as the preferred approach. It follows the World Bank Proxy Means Test standard, used by DSWD to target the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program (4Ps). Its transparent, linear coefficients reveal precisely which household characteristics predict welfare — making it the ideal tool for policy targeting.

PMT Ridge Regression

Education, geography, and infrastructure drive the ranking

The Ridge PMT assigns a single linear welfare score to each household — a transparent, explainable number. The top 15 coefficients reveal what matters most for Philippine welfare. Head's tertiary education ranks first (+0.084), confirming the premium on higher learning. Six of the top 15 predictors are region dummies — a striking reminder that geography shapes welfare as powerfully as individual household choices. The remaining top predictors are infrastructure and assets: car ownership, flush toilets, piped water, electricity, and the ability to save financially.

Negative coefficients for Central Visayas, Davao Region, and North Mindanao signal systematic disadvantage in these areas — controlling for all other factors. Meanwhile, Negros Island and Eastern Visayas rank higher. This regional variation, frozen in the PMT coefficients, becomes the backbone of spatially-targeted poverty interventions.

The welfare gap is substantial and visible. Moving from the poorest quintile (Q1) to the wealthiest (Q5), households accumulate assets steadily. Car ownership shows the steepest gradient: Q5 households are nearly 10 times more likely to own a car than Q1 (90% vs 9%). Access to piped water rises from 35% in Q1 to 83% in Q5. Even electricity, seemingly universal at first glance, reveals a 16.5 percentage point gap between Q1 (84%) and Q5 (100%).

These asset gradients are not random. They reflect cumulative economic advantage. Q1 households lack not one asset but many, suggesting a "poverty syndrome" where disadvantage clusters across multiple dimensions simultaneously.

Geographic Welfare

NCR leads, Visayas lags — a 1.15 SD gap

The geographic welfare gradient in the Philippines is one of the sharpest facts in the data. The National Capital Region (NCR) households score +0.739 standard deviations above the national mean. Visayas households score −0.413 SD below — a gap of 1.15 standard deviations. To put this in perspective: the urban-rural divide, often cited as the axis of Philippine inequality, is only 0.65 SD. Geography overwhelms urbanization.

Region dummies in the PMT model capture these differences with precision. Central Visayas (reg_7) and Davao Region (reg_11) reduce predicted welfare scores. Eastern Visayas (reg_8) and Negros Island (reg_18) raise them. This is not measurement noise — it reflects real, structural economic disadvantage in certain provinces, above and beyond individual household characteristics.

The pattern holds in simpler terms: 77% of Q5 households are in Luzon (including NCR), while 61% of Q1 households are scattered across Visayas and Mindanao. Distribution shapes destiny.

Robustness Check

Five methods, one consistent picture

Five radically different statistical approaches produced five different welfare rankings. Yet when we compare them pairwise, they converge. Method 1 (Core PCA) and Method 2 (Extended PCA) agree 74% of the time — they place the same household in the same quintile. Method 1 and the Ridge PMT (Method 5) agree 40% of the time, which may sound low until you realize: assigning 2,470 households to 5 quintiles by random chance would yield agreement only 20% of the time. The PMT agrees with PCA twice as often as chance, a meaningful signal that both capture the same underlying welfare dimension.

More revealing: at least 3 of the 5 methods assign the same quintile for 73.5% of households. For one-third of the sample, all 4 or 5 methods converge. This suggests the underlying welfare dimension is robust — the data speaks louder than methodology.

Recommendation

Which ranking to use

We recommend Method 5 (Ridge PMT) as the primary welfare ranking for the L2Phl analysis. Here is why: it offers transparent linear coefficients, has been cross-validated against overfitting, includes geographic variables critical for spatial targeting, and follows World Bank Proxy Means Test standards used by DSWD for 4Ps beneficiary selection. For the Philippines, this is the natural choice for poverty policy.

But each of the five methods serves a purpose. Core PCA (M1) is location-free and internationally comparable — ideal for benchmarking against DHS surveys and other countries' welfare indices. The Random Forest (M3), though it overfits, can identify nonlinear patterns for robustness checking. The consensus quintile offers a median estimate when uncertainty is high.
