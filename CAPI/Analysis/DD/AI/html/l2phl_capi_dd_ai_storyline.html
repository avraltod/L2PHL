01The Headline Number
National Prevalence

One in Two Filipino Households Worries AI Will Replace Jobs

When asked whether they worry that artificial intelligence and new technology will replace jobs, 52.4 percent of Filipino households agreed or agreed with conviction. That places AI anxiety ahead of concerns about job loss from economic downturns (56.8%) and close behind worries about politics (63.7%).

The distribution is not uniformly alarmed. About one in five households (20.2%) sit on the fence, while 27.4 percent disagree or disagree with conviction. The median Filipino household, in other words, leans anxious but not panicked.

More than half the country expresses AI-related job anxiety, yet no national AI workforce transition program exists.

02Concern Landscape
All 11 V9 Statements Compared

AI Worry Ranks 10th of 11 V9 Statements

Citizens having a say in government (77.2%) and rising prices (75.5%) draw the highest agreement across the 11-item V9 battery. AI anxiety sits at 52.4 percent, above only the statement on taxes being well-spent (36.6%). Note that the battery mixes negative concerns (prices, job loss, AI) with positive assessments (good time to find a job, optimism about the economy, digital services), so the ranking is not a pure concern hierarchy.

V9f (education worry) is asked only of households with school-age children and thus has a different denominator from the other 10 items. Among parents, agreement reaches 70.8 percent. On the full sample (coding non-parents as "not worried"), the figure drops to 46.1 percent, which would place education worry below AI anxiety. The chart shows the parent-only figure to reflect intensity among the relevant population, but readers should note the different base.

03Who Worries Most
Demographics

The U-Shaped Income Pattern: Lowest and Highest Income Groups Worry Most

AI anxiety does not follow a simple rich-poor gradient. Households self-rated in the lowest income class register 57.0 percent anxiety, while those in the highest class reach 61.1 percent, while the "above average" income class dips to just 36.7 percent. This U-shape suggests two distinct anxieties: poorer households may fear displacement from low-skill automation, while wealthier households, who are more exposed to information about AI, may anticipate disruption in professional roles.

By age, anxiety peaks among HH heads aged 35–44 (54.9%) and falls to 48.0 percent for those over 65, who may feel less labour-market threat. Male-headed households register 52.8 percent and female-headed 51.1 percent; this 1.7 percentage-point gap is too small to be distinguishable from sampling variability at conventional confidence levels.

Education: A Non-Linear Gradient

Secondary-educated household heads register the lowest anxiety at 45.6 percent, while primary-educated heads reach 64.0 percent. College and TVET graduates rebound to 54.6 percent, and post-graduates settle at 52.3 percent. The primary-educated group's high anxiety aligns with their greater vulnerability to routine-task automation. The small cell of HH heads with less than primary education (100%) should be treated as unreliable given the limited sample size in that category.

04Geography of Anxiety
Regional Patterns

Visayas Highest at 57.7%, NCR at 51.5%

Visayas registers the highest point estimate at 57.7 percent, followed by Mindanao at 54.4 percent. Luzon excluding NCR sits lowest at 49.4 percent. The 8.3 percentage-point gap between Visayas and Luzon (excl. NCR) is the largest regional difference; while formal significance tests have not been run, the magnitude is large enough to warrant attention. NCR, the national capital and BPO hub, sits at 51.5 percent, close to the national average despite higher exposure to AI-driven service-sector automation.

The urban-rural split is negligible: 53.1 percent urban versus 51.5 percent rural. The 1.6 percentage-point difference is not statistically distinguishable from zero at conventional confidence levels, indicating that AI anxiety has diffused well beyond metro centres.

05Work & Contracts
Labour Market

Informal Workers Report Slightly Higher Anxiety

Employed household heads register 53.6 percent anxiety compared with 49.6 percent among those not working, a 4.0 percentage-point gap that is directionally consistent with labour-market exposure but falls in the borderline range for statistical significance given the sample size. Among the employed, those with no formal contract register 54.0 percent, verbal contracts 53.0 percent, and written contracts 50.7 percent. These differences of 1 to 3 percentage points are too narrow to confirm a statistically significant contract-type gradient.

Even so, the pattern is directionally coherent. Informal workers, who make up 71.8 percent of the employed workforce, lack the institutional buffers (severance, retraining clauses, union protections) that might temper anxiety about displacement. Whether or not the point estimates reach significance, the policy implication holds: the most exposed workers also have the fewest protections.

06Digital Divide
Connectivity & Awareness

Internet Access Shows No Clear Divide on AI Anxiety

Offline households register 53.9 percent AI anxiety versus 50.8 percent for those with internet access, a 3.1 percentage-point difference. This gap is not statistically significant at conventional confidence levels given the sample size, so we cannot confirm that connectivity is associated with lower anxiety. The point estimates are directionally consistent with a story in which connected households have more first-hand exposure to technology that augments rather than replaces work, but the data do not allow us to distinguish this pattern from chance.

Regardless of whether the gap is real, the absolute levels are notable: both groups hover above 50 percent. Digital literacy programmes, which 64.9 percent of AI-anxious households support, could help ground AI perceptions in practical experience for connected and unconnected households alike.

07Financial Resilience
Vulnerability

AI Anxiety Cuts Across Financial Lines

Households that can cover an emergency and those that cannot register nearly identical AI anxiety rates: 52.0 percent versus 52.4 percent. Likewise, life satisfaction averages 3.04 on a 5-point scale for both AI-anxious and non-anxious groups. AI anxiety does not appear to be a proxy for general economic distress. It is a distinct, technology-specific concern that cuts across income and financial-resilience categories.

This distinction matters for policy design. Interventions aimed at reducing AI anxiety cannot rely solely on social protection or cash transfer programmes. The concern runs deeper, into questions of occupational identity, career trajectory, and the pace of technological change.

08Policy Demand
What People Want

AI-Anxious Filipinos Are More Interventionist Across the Board

AI-anxious households are 7 to 15 percentage points more likely to support every policy proposition in the survey. The widest gap is on limiting imported goods: 67.8 percent of anxious households support it versus 53.0 percent of those not anxious, a 14.8 percentage-point gap. Green energy and recycling as a top priority draws the broadest support overall (75.1% among the anxious). Raising taxes for better public services is the least popular proposition at 36.4 percent, though even here the anxious group is 7.5 points more supportive.

09Global Context
External Evidence

The Philippines Has the Highest AI Job Exposure in ASEAN

The demand-side anxiety captured in L2Phl aligns with supply-side estimates from international organisations. An IMF working paper (Cucio and Hennig, WP/2025/043, February 2025) maps occupational AI exposure across the Philippines and finds that around one third of workers are in highly AI-exposed occupations, with 61 percent of those also rated highly complementary. Separately, an ILO research brief published in February 2026 estimates 12.7 million Filipino workers (more than one quarter of employment) are exposed to generative AI, the highest rate among ASEAN countries with comparable data, though only 3.6 percent of jobs fall into the highest displacement-risk category. The BPO sector, which employs roughly 1.7 million Filipinos, faces particular exposure to generative AI tools that can handle customer service, data entry, and content moderation.

A World Bank estimate puts 35 to 37 percent of Philippine jobs at some level of AI risk. Globally, the Ipsos AI Monitor 2024 finds that 52 percent of respondents across 32 countries feel nervous about AI products and services, up 12 percentage points in two years. The Pew Research Center (February 2025) reports that 52 percent of U.S. workers worry about AI's future workplace impact. The L2Phl figure of 52.4 percent is thus consistent with international patterns.

Yet the Philippines has no national AI policy framework and no dedicated workforce transition programme for AI-displaced workers. The 52.4 percent anxiety rate is, in this context, consistent with the structural exposure estimates and the absence of a policy response.

10Key Takeaways
So What

Five Findings for Policymakers

1. AI anxiety is a majority concern. At 52.4 percent, it has moved from a niche worry to a mainstream sentiment shared by more than half of households.

2. The U-shaped income gradient spans the full distribution. Households in the lowest (57.0%) and highest (61.1%) self-rated income classes show the most anxiety, consistent with both low-skill displacement concern and professional-role disruption concern operating simultaneously.

3. Informality and anxiety: directional but not confirmed. Workers without written contracts register 54.0 percent anxiety versus 50.7 percent for those with formal agreements, a gap that is directionally coherent but not statistically significant at conventional confidence levels. The policy case for extending labour protections to informal workers rests on their structural vulnerability, not on a confirmed anxiety differential.

4. No clear internet divide. Offline households register 53.9 percent anxiety versus 50.8 percent for connected ones, a difference that is not statistically distinguishable from zero. Both groups sit above 50 percent, underscoring that AI anxiety is pervasive regardless of connectivity status.

5. AI-anxious households lean interventionist. They are 7–15 percentage points more likely than the non-anxious to support trade protection (67.8%), green energy (75.1%), government digital services (64.9%), and state direction of the economy (59.8%). The pattern points to a broader demand for institutional responses to economic uncertainty.
