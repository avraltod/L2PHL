Module 08 · Food & Nutrition
Food Security — The Biggest Story of Recovery
FIES trend

Moderate-to-severe food insecurity fell from 41% to 18% in five months

The Food Insecurity Experience Scale (FIES) asks households five progressively severe questions — from worry about food to going an entire day without eating. When 3 or more are answered "yes," the household is classified as moderately-to-severely food insecure.

In Round 1, 41% of households crossed that threshold. By Round 5, that figure had fallen to 18.2% — a drop of 23 percentage points. Severe food insecurity (all 5 items yes) fell from an estimated 6–8% to 3.7%. This is the clearest positive signal in the entire CATI dataset.

Mindanao and Visayas remain the most food-insecure macro-regions: 23% and 23% moderate-severe respectively in Round 5. NCR is lowest at 9%.

52.4% of households are now food secure (FIES=0) in Round 5. In Round 1, fewer than 6 in 10 were fully food secure.

FIES items

39% worried about food. 6.5% went a whole day without eating.

The most prevalent food insecurity experience in Round 5 is worry: 39.2% of households worried about not having enough food because of money or resources. This is the "entry" level of food stress.

At the severe end: 14.8% were hungry but did not eat, and 6.5% went an entire day without food. These are not abstract worries — they represent real hunger affecting millions of Filipino households even after five months of improvement.

Mindanao registers the sharpest vulnerability: 49.1% worried about food vs. 24% in NCR. Visayas closely tracks Mindanao at 47.3%.

Module 03 · Shocks & Resilience
Shocks — From 35% to 12% Over Five Months
Shock incidence

The shock burden fell sharply — but illness remains the top driver

In Round 1, 34.9% of households reported being hit by an economic or environmental shock in the past 30 days. By Round 5, only 12.1% reported a shock — a two-thirds reduction. This reflects both seasonal patterns and genuine resilience-building.

Major illness (4.5%) is the single largest shock type in Round 5, followed by typhoon exposure (2.5%) and unexpected job loss (1.8%). Visayas is worst-affected at 18.4%, while Mindanao is lowest at 2.1%.

Water disruption affected 9.8% of households in Round 5, with an average of 4.2 days disrupted. Electricity outages averaged 13.9 hours in the past week — a persistent service quality gap. Internet disruption in the past month was reported by 29.7%.

Module 06 · Finance
Finance — Mobile Leads, Banks Trail
Financial access (R5)

Mobile money at 50%. Bank accounts at 12%. Online shopping rising fast.

Half of Filipino households now have a mobile money account (GCash, Maya or equivalent) — up sharply from the CAPI baseline of 18%. The gap between mobile money (50%) and formal bank accounts (12%) has never been wider, and it tells a story of fintech reaching where traditional banking hasn't.

34.2% used a bank in the past 30 days, and 41.7% used a mobile wallet for a transaction. 23.6% bought something online — a notable jump suggesting digital commerce is entering the mainstream even in lower-income households.

18.1% of households took a loan in the past 30 days. The top purpose: medical and health expenses (30%), housing repair (25%), and business needs (12%). Informal lenders remain common (16% of loan sources) despite the fintech growth.

Only 3.5% of households could absorb a ₱300,000 emergency expense. The financial buffer gap remains severe even as mobile access grows.

Module 07 · Health
Health — PhilHealth at 55%, but Out-of-Pocket is the Norm
Health access (R5)

54.9% have PhilHealth — but 59% pay out-of-pocket for consultations

PhilHealth coverage reaches 54.9% of the panel — a meaningful improvement from the CAPI baseline of 46.1%. NCR leads at 71.8%, while Luzon (excl. NCR) trails at 52.3%. Only 4.8% of households reported needing health care in the past 30 days, and 88.9% of those who needed it managed to access it.

Despite coverage gains, out-of-pocket payments remain endemic: 59.3% of those who consulted paid cash, with a mean outlay of ₱1,198 — modest per visit but catastrophic when repeated. Hospitalization hit 5.8% of members, with a mean bill of ₱36,717 and ₱9,009 out-of-pocket.

The most-used facility is the Barangay Health Station (37%), followed by public hospitals (34%). Private options serve just 16% of visits.

Modules 04–05 · Employment & Income
Employment — 34% Rate, Informality Dominates
Labour market (R4–R5)

Employment at 34% — 68.6% have no contract

Employment rate in rounds 4–5 is 34–36% (using the composite emp_status indicator). This measures formal employment engagement rather than all work activity. Workers average 39.3 hours per week — full-time for those employed.

Job quality remains fragile: 68.6% of employed workers have no contract, only 18.4% have a written agreement. Employer pension contributions cover just 16.4% of workers. Gig work is rare at 0.7% — most informal work is traditional rather than platform-based.

Private household work (29%) and private establishment employment (27%) are the top job types. Self-employment stands at 18.3%. Government jobs provide security for 7.5%.

Module 09 · Views & Perceptions
Voices — Prices, Stability, and Cautious Hope
Perceptions (R5)

52% say situation is the same. 44% say prices are rising too fast.

Life satisfaction averages 2.88 out of 5 (where 1 is most satisfied) — almost identical across all five rounds, ranging only from 2.79 to 2.88. The panel's wellbeing is broadly stable despite food and shock improvements. Economic security may be improving, but subjective satisfaction has not moved.

52.4% say their economic situation is the same as last month. 23.9% say it is somewhat worse, and 8.4% say much worse. Only 15.3% report any improvement. Mindanao is most stable (55.5% same) while Visayas is most pessimistic (40.8% worse in some degree).

Price anxiety is the sharpest signal: 44.5% agree prices are rising too fast — the highest agreement rate. At the same time, 40.3% believe the country is on the right track, and 38.7% trust taxes are well spent. These positives coexist with 33.7% worried about job loss.

"Recovery is measurable. Vulnerability is not gone."

Five months of calls tell a story of real but fragile improvement. Food stress halved. Shock incidence collapsed. PhilHealth coverage grew. And yet: 18% are still moderately food insecure. 59% still pay out-of-pocket when they get sick. 69% of workers have no contract. Price anxiety is the sharpest signal from every round. The improvement is real — but the floor remains low.
