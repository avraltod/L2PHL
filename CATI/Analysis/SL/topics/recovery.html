Theme 1 · Food · Shocks

Recovery is measurable

When the panel began in November, OLD of households were cutting meals or eating less because they could not afford enough food. That share fell through the winter, to about 19 percent by March. The decline then stalled and partly reversed. By Round 8 food insecurity stood at OLD, a drop of OLD points from the first round but above the March low. The data suggest a recovery that is real and unfinished.

A national average hides where hunger sits. Broken out by region, the chart separates into two bands. Metro Manila and the rest of Luzon began near a third of households and fell into the high teens. The Visayas and Mindanao began above half and, despite the steepest declines, stayed near a quarter through Round 8.

The recovery reached every region. It did not close the distance between them.

Exposure to shocks follows the same arc. In the first round, OLD of households reported a recent shock — a lost job, an illness, a storm, a price spike. That share fell to OLD by Round 8, with the same winter dip and spring rebound. Food stress and shocks move together, because a shock that cuts a household's income is also what pushes it to cut meals.
